# Outstanding Brand Decisions

Decisions still needed following the founder branding session (24 Feb 2026). These should be resolved before finalising the website redesign.

---

## 1. Exact colour palette

**Context:** All three founders agreed on "refined orange" as the direction, rejecting bright/garish Monzo-style coral. Zoe strongly prefers orange-only palette (no strong secondary colour like teal). But no specific hex values or palette were locked.

**Sub-questions:**
- **Primary orange tone:** How muted/warm/dark should it be? The actual colour "amber" came up at the end of the session — a golden-brown-orange that's darker and warmer than the current brand orange. Steph and Zoe both associated the word "amber" with this darker, more precious tone. Is this the right direction, or do we stay closer to the current orange?
- **Accent colours:** If no strong secondary colour, what provides visual contrast for secondary buttons, subheadings, highlights? Zoe suggested grays and grayscale work well alongside orange. Jamie flagged that designing without a contrasting accent is harder. Need to see this in practice.
- **Background colour:** The off-white in the concepts was flagged as both "a tad too corporate" (Zoe) and "too similar to Claude" (Steph). What's the right warm neutral that feels like us and not like Anthropic?

**Decision needed:** Finalise a colour palette (primary, accent shades, backgrounds, text colours) and validate it doesn't overlap with Claude/Anthropic.

---

## 2. Claude/Anthropic brand differentiation

**Context:** Steph raised this multiple times with genuine concern. The Concept B mockup (off-white background + orange accent + black text) was "exactly the same" as Claude's brand in her view. She said: "We definitely wouldn't want people to be like, 'Oh, are they linked to Claude?'"

**Decision needed:** How specifically do we differentiate? Options include:
- Shifting the background colour (warmer? cooler? slightly different tone?)
- Using a distinctly different orange (more amber/golden vs. Claude's terracotta?)
- Different typographic choices that create distance
- Need to look at Claude's actual palette side-by-side and identify the minimum viable divergence

---

## 3. Logo direction

**Context:** Explicitly deferred during the session. Jamie told everyone to "ignore the logo in the top left" for all concepts. The current logo will need revisiting as part of the brand refresh.

**Decision needed:** Commission/design a new logo, or refine the existing one? Does the word "Ambr" stay as-is? Does "AI" stay in the name or get dropped/de-emphasised? (The consensus to not "big up AI" may have implications here.)

---

## 4. Photography and imagery strategy

**Context:** Concept B (the winner) was entirely text-forward with no photography or illustrations in the hero. The team responded positively to this minimalism. But a full website needs visual content beyond typography.

**Decision needed:**
- Does the site use photography at all? If so, what style? (The current brand has photography — is that retained or replaced?)
- Are illustrations used? (The session was told to "ignore the illustrations" in concepts)
- How do we add visual texture without reverting to startup-y stock imagery or generic AI visuals?

---



## 6. The memorability question

**Context:** Steph's biggest fear was "forgettable" — a concern neither Jamie nor Zoe shared as strongly. With the move toward a more sophisticated, muted, Harvey-inspired direction, the risk of being forgettable arguably increases. Zoe acknowledged the tension: "If you were optimising for [not forgettable], I think you'd go for basically probably what we have now — a really bright colour."

**Decision needed:** What specifically makes the brand memorable in the Harvey-style direction? Candidates:
- The quality and confidence of the typography itself
- Distinctive use of white space (unusual in this market)
- The amber colour being genuinely different from competitor palettes
- Strong, proof-heavy copy that sticks because of substance, not style
- Something else entirely?

If we can't articulate what makes us memorable in this direction, we may need to recalibrate.

---

## 7. Dark/contrast sections

**Context:** Zoe specifically disliked the black background section in Concept B, calling it "too harsh." But websites need visual rhythm and section breaks.

**Decision needed:** How do we create contrast between page sections without dark backgrounds? Options:
- Subtle background colour shifts (very light warm tones)
- Border/divider treatments
- Typography scale changes
- Something else?

---

## 8. Competitive visual positioning

**Context:** Jamie noted competitors use garish, gradient, startup-y aesthetics (Hyperbound, Yoodli). The Harvey direction would create strong differentiation. But nobody has done a systematic visual comparison.

**Decision needed:** Do a visual audit of key competitors' websites and confirm the Harvey-inspired direction creates sufficient differentiation. Steph framed this well: "We want people to be like, okay yeah, I'm looking at Ambr and Yoodli on my browser, two tabs — what's the vibe of both?"

---

## 9. How far to take the "quiet confidence" aesthetic

**Context:** The team agreed on Concept B but Zoe added the caveat "maybe with a touch of C" — meaning the muted orange gradients and warmer accent tones from Concept C, because "B might be a tiny bit too far on the authoritative spectrum."

**Decision needed:** Calibrate the exact position. This will be answered in practice through design iterations, but the team should review the first real mockup specifically through this lens: "Is this too cold? Too warm? Right in the middle?"

---

3. **Copy voice rules** (needed before writing any page content)
4. **Photography/imagery strategy** (needed before designing below-the-fold sections)
5. **Memorability mechanism** (philosophical but shapes every design choice)
